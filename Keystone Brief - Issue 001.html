I. The Decision Framework — Cut Through Analysis Paralysis

Before you buy another AI tool, run it through this filter. It's adapted from the HBR decision framework and validated against the most common SMB purchasing mistakes.

The 4-question AI vendor filter

1. What specific, measurable problem does this solve?

If the answer is "improve efficiency" or "stay competitive," walk away. The right answer names a process, a current cost, and a target saving. Example: "Reduce customer support response time from 4 hours to 15 minutes for Tier 1 tickets" — not "transform your customer experience."

2. Can I test this with my actual data before committing?

Any vendor that won't do a proof-of-concept with your real data is selling a dream. The average SMB found that AI tools failed when exposed to messy real-world data — not clean demo environments. If they won't let you break it before you buy it, don't buy it.

3. What is the total cost including training and change management?

Multiple SMB owners reported that training staff to use AI tools cost more than the software itself. One manufacturing ops director: "My workers are 55 years old, been here 20 years. They don't want AI. Training costs more than the software." Include a line item for training hours in your evaluation.

4. Can I cancel without penalty and get my data out?

This is the single best filter for overhyped vendors. If the contract has lock-in terms or data export fees, they are not confident you'll stay because of the value. The best AI tools compete on results, not on exit barriers.

II. Regulatory Reality — What You Actually Need to Worry About

62% of SMB leaders fear AI regulation. Here's what actually applies to you.

NSBA 2024 Survey

The most common SMB regulatory fear: "Am I one bad AI output away from a lawsuit?" The honest answer is it depends on what you're doing with AI. Let me save you hours of anxiety by breaking it down into three tiers:

Tier 1 — Act now (high risk): You use AI for hiring, credit decisions, insurance underwriting, or biometrics (facial recognition, fingerprint analysis). Colorado's AI Act directly applies. EU AI Act directly applies if you process EU persons. Budget $2,000-5,000 for legal review — manageable if you do it now, punishing if you wait.

Tier 2 — Watch (medium risk): You use AI for customer service chatbots, content generation, internal analytics, or CRM features. You need basic disclosure (tell customers they're interacting with AI) and human oversight procedures. This is a policy document, not a legal project.

Tier 3 — Ignore (low risk): You use generic office productivity AI (spell check, calendar scheduling, meeting transcription). No current regulation touches this. Do not let a consultant convince you otherwise.

Canada just published a national AI strategy. It's the template for US states.

The New York Times · June 4, 2026

Canada's strategy includes consumer protection rules, mandated transparency, and a new enforcement body. Colorado's AI Act is already law. California's draft bill is expected to pass mid-2027. The regulatory trajectory is clear: disclosure, documentation, and human oversight will become mandatory for any AI system that makes consequential decisions about people.

The practical takeaway: If you are in Tier 1 above, hire a lawyer this quarter. If you are in Tier 2, write a one-page AI use policy this month. The template costs you nothing and protects you from the 62% anxiety stat that keeps SMB leaders up at night.
III. Market Signal — What the SpaceX-Google Deal Tells You About Your Budget

Google is paying SpaceX $920 million per month for AI compute. Your tools will cost more in 2026.

The New York Times · June 5, 2026

Google — one of the largest AI companies on earth — cannot buy enough compute capacity domestically and is buying it from a rocket company instead. This is the single strongest signal that AI infrastructure is structurally constrained.

What to do this week: Ask your top 3 AI vendors one question: "Do you own your training and inference infrastructure, or do you rent it?" If they rent, expect a 15-25% price increase in the next 12 months. Have a switching plan. This is not fear-mongering — it is the direct consequence of a $30 billion compute deal that tells you supply cannot keep up with demand.

Reid Hoffman leaves Microsoft board. Your Copilot strategy needs a Plan B.

The New York Times · June 5, 2026

Hoffman was the personal bridge between Satya Nadella and Sam Altman. His departure from Microsoft's board signals that the structural tension between Microsoft (wants platform lock-in) and OpenAI (wants independence) is nearing a breaking point.

Do not panic. Do diversify. If your AI workflows are built on Microsoft Copilot or Azure OpenAI services, ensure they can run on standard APIs (OpenAI's direct API, Anthropic's API) without rewriting. Platform lock-in is the single most expensive AI mistake you can make this year — it turns a vendor disagreement into a six-figure migration project.
IV. What to Ignore This Week
Every LinkedIn message from an "AI strategy consultant" you've never heard of. A recurring SMB pain point from Reddit: cold-inmail AI consultants charging $5k-$20k for strategy decks that are templates. The real AI consultants are too busy building things to cold-message you. Your $297/month subscription to this brief is a better investment than a $5,000 deck from a stranger.
"AI will replace your job" think pieces. Every automation wave has produced these. They have never been right. The NYT's feature on SMBs deploying "armies of AI agents" is instructive: the owners are still running their businesses. AI replaces tasks, not judgment.
Any "AI-powered" upgrade to software you already own. Multiple SMB owners reported spending $5k-$20k on CRM AI add-ons that "did nothing useful." If your existing software vendor is offering an AI upgrade, ask: "Can I try it for 30 days at no cost, and can I revert to the old version?" If the answer to either is no, decline.
